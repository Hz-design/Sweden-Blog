What is?

Location scouting is a vital component in the pre-production stage of filmmaking and commercial photography. Once producers or directors decide a scene outside of the studio is needed, the search for a stuiable place or “location” begins. Location scouts eek out spectacular or intersting locations beforehand, to have a database of locations in case of requests: Locamundo.com/en location scout website (exclusive to location scouts)

How to scout

A location scout tpically takes discriptive, a panoramic photography or video of locatino possibilities. A good location scout will make photos of a location possibility that teflect the aesthetic goals of the prodictopm Include visually descriptive photography and information to present, documenting more than what will potentially appear onscreen. (What’s on the other side of the wall, where can you put your equipment?) A scout als has to consider practical information. Does your location have easy access? Parking? Noise? Bathrooms? Food ? Power? … (what’s the escape route for the location incase of a fire, is there food nearby? Is there enough power? Where is the nearest area to plug in ?) Is there a suitable holding are for talent and crew? (room space corner, for a temp offfice, eg. people working with laptop he needs to work in silent, make up artist in their own environment) Whilst scouting find contact information. Who owns the property? Photograph clues for when you need to secure permissions later. (Park outside, related to the people living there?, License plate number you can freely find out who the owner of the car is, providing you with information, you can easily trackdown people) MAke sure you are collecting Geo Data either embedded in your images or using a map application. (Use your phone to make a photo for geo data, because not all dslr’s have the option) (How’s the light working?, where’s the light coming from? usually you don;t want sun coming from)

Wat do you need for scouting?

A scout would carry a camera (HR), weather protection, Extra memory, Extra battery, Extra camera, Compass(your phone), Tape measure and tripod. A camera (High resolution) doesn’t have to be an expensive camera. Your phone is probably enough. The ability to change lensen is a bonus however. (good wide angle lensen) Weather protection: Keep you and your camera dry. Wrap up warm… Layers, layers, layers. (Never to cold, never to hot, you only got the wrong clothes). Memory cards come in many capacities and speeds. Use what you can afford, carry a spare, format the card with your camera. Extra battery: fully charged before you leave. A power bank is a must if using your phone. Extra camera: hope for the best…. prepare for the worst. Compass: knowing the sun position in relation your location will help you plan your shoot. Browser and app version: The photographer’s ephemeris (where’s the moon is coming to be, do you want the light of the moon?) Tape Measuer; a shoot often require space. It’s handy to know basic room measurements. Tripod; will help keep your camera steady and level.

Style

Location scout photos are often used to inform about a location. (There are all of these disturbance objects, how, who removes them? Do people come regulary to the location to do things?) As a good an overview of a location as possible is necessary. The object is to be descriptive not artistic. Horizontal not vertical. Be sure to capture images that overlap. Look for prominent object thjat are easy to identify.

Tools

- Software such as TPE allows you to chart the sun and moon positions. - Apps such as Sun Seeker use AR to visualise sun and moon positions. - Photopills.com If you are scouting with your phone and intend to shoot with a professional camera later try a viewfinder app. Magic canonic viewFinder. The viewfinder app enables you to visualise the crop your final shot might have. Google maps can used to build custom maps, you can pin locations and add media, collaborate or share easily.

The music video for The confusions was shot entirely on location scheduling for the video relied on the data gathered during the scout. (the sun can be used for a good back light, be cautios how much time you have) The availability of light influenced the shooting schedule and informed the equipment list. Confusion - Shooting plan The centrepiece for the video was a performance in the library using the window as a backlight. This required perfect timing and a bit of luck with the weather. Close ups of the musicians used a synthethic light to fake the sun once it had passed the window.

Case study II

The short film The Good Smaaritan, was shot outdoors. The story demanded a car, stuck in snow on a desolated road. We created a map and went exploring. Apparently forest pretty much looks the same no matter how far you drive. Maria has made up her mind that this location isn’t up to scratch, meanwhile Jonathan has found another tree of interest. The best of the locations we foudn that day didn’t have sufficient resources nearby for a two day shoot in winter conditions. (parked my own car to how it looked on the shot, there were no places nearby for food, etc.)(So we still had no location) Eeventually our producer suggested a location that ticked all the right boxes Elevation, Isolation, Photogenic and facilities nearby. Once a location was found a storyboard was made that fit the location. Because our collective drwawing skills were rubbish I revisted the location with Henrik (the first AC) to previsualise. Revisting the location enabled us to figure out camera positions and lenses. (A map of camera’s with numbers, part of the script. Take a drone up their to take a photo of that. The material we shot allowed us to visualise the look and feel of the film.

summary

- Scouting is surveying and documenting - Scouting photography is informative, not artistic. - Be prepared iwth the right equipment, have a plan B. - Shoot horizontal, shoot form the hip. - Keep in mind the practical needs of crew such as food and water. - Use apps to log, capture and plan ahead. - Consider lighting problems and workarounds.
